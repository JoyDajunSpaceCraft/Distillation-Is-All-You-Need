
Initial Diagnostic Question

Question: What steps should be taken when a symptomatic bone lesion is present and the X-ray is abnormal?
Answer: The next step in the diagnostic pathway depends on the patient's age.
Age-Based Diagnostic Decision

Follow-Up Question: What is the age of the patient?

If the patient is under 40 years old

Question: What should be done if the patient is under 40 years of age?
Answer: The patient should be referred to an orthopedic oncologist, and a biopsy should be performed at the treating institution if indicated.
If the patient is 40 years of age or older

Question: What should be done if the patient is 40 years of age or older?
Answer: Further tests should be conducted to determine if bone metastasis is present.
Further Testing Question: What tests should be performed to determine if bone metastasis is present?
Further Testing Answer: These tests include history and physical examination, bone scan or FDG-PET/CT, chest X-ray, serum protein electrophoresis (SPEP), mammogram, chest/abdominal/pelvic (C/A/P) CT with contrast, prostate-specific antigen (PSA), and other lab tests.
Follow-Up Tests for Bone Metastasis

If no other lesions are found
Question: What should be done if no other lesions are found?
Answer: The patient should be referred to an orthopedic oncologist for a biopsy.
If signs of other non-bone primary lesions are found
Question: What should be done if signs of other non-bone primary lesions are found during the examination?
Answer: Treatment should follow the NCCN guidelines for the specific type of cancer.
Atypical Cartilaginous Tumors

Question: Which treatment guidelines should be followed for atypical cartilaginous tumors?
Answer: The CHON-2 treatment guidelines should be followed for atypical cartilaginous tumors.
CHON-2 Guidelines:
Primary Treatment: Recommended treatments include intralesional excision and potential surgical adjuvant therapy, or observation.
Surveillance: Physical exam, X-rays of the primary site and/or cross-sectional imaging every 6–12 months for 2 years, then annually early, with CT with contrast or MRI as clinically indicated.
Recurrence: For local recurrence, perform imaging of the primary site as indicated (e.g., X-ray, MRI, CT with contrast), and if there is malignant transformation, follow the CHON-3 guidelines.
Workup: Upon recurrence, continue with imaging studies and if there is malignant transformation, treat according to CHON-3 guidelines.
Treatment for Specific Chondrosarcoma Types

Question: How should low-grade extracompartmental appendicular tumors, grade I axial tumors, high-grade tumors, clear cell tumors, or extracompartmental tumors be treated?
Answer: These types of tumors should be treated according to the CHON-3 treatment guidelines.
CHON-3 Guidelines:
Primary Treatment: Wide excision if resectable, or consider radiation therapy if borderline resectable or unresectable.
Surveillance: Conduct physical exams and imaging (X-ray, CT, MRI) every 3–6 months for 5 years, then annually, reassess function at every visit.
Recurrence: Wide excision is preferred for local recurrence if resectable. Consider radiation therapy if the tumor is borderline resectable or unresectable. Follow CHON-4 guidelines for systemic recurrence.
Metastatic Disease and Dedifferentiated Tumors

Question: How should metastatic disease at presentation or dedifferentiated tumors be treated?
Answer: Treat according to the CHON-4 treatment guidelines.
CHON-4 Guidelines for Metastatic Chondrosarcoma:
Dedifferentiated and Mesenchymal: Treatment should be considered according to the EW-1 guidelines.
Oligometastatic Disease: Aim for surgical excision of all metastatic sites if possible, consider radiation therapy for unresectable sites, and participation in clinical trials.
Widespread Disease: Consider radiation therapy, surgery, and/or ablative therapies for symptomatic sites, with systemic therapy and clinical trial participation as options.
Mesenchymal Tumors

Question: How should mesenchymal tumors be treated?
Answer: Treat in accordance with the NCCN category 2B guidelines for Ewing's sarcoma, designated as EW-1.
Diagnostic Workup for Chordoma

Question: What are the steps included in the diagnostic workup for chordoma?
Answer: The diagnostic workup includes evaluation and treatment by a multidisciplinary team, history and physical examination, adequate cross-sectional imaging of the primary site, C/A/P CT with contrast, consideration of FDG-PET/CT scan, and bone scan if FDG-PET/CT is negative.
Histologic Subtype

Conventional or Chondroid Chordoma
Question: Which guideline should be referred to for the presentation and initial treatment of conventional or chondroid chordoma?
Answer: The guidelines for the presentation and primary treatment (CHOR-2) should be referred to.
Poorly Differentiated or Dedifferentiated Chordoma
Question: Which guidelines should be referred to for poorly differentiated or dedifferentiated chordoma?
Answer: The NCCN guidelines for soft tissue sarcoma should be referred to.
Chordoma Treatment Guidelines

Sacrococcygeal and Mobile Spine Chordoma
Question: Is the sacrococcygeal or mobile spine chordoma resectable?
Answers:
If yes: Perform wide resection and consider radiation therapy if needed.
If no: Consider radiation therapy as the tumor is unresectable.
Skull Base Clival Chordoma
Question: Is the skull base or clival chordoma resectable?
Answers:
If yes: Perform intralesional excision and consider radiation therapy if needed.
If no: Consider radiation therapy as the tumor is unresectable.
Surveillance

Question: How should surveillance for chordoma be conducted?
Answer: Regular physical examinations, imaging of the primary site as clinically indicated, chest imaging every 6 months for 5 years, then annually thereafter as clinically indicated.
Recurrence

Local Recurrence
Question: How should a local recurrence of chordoma be treated?
Answer: Surgical excision, radiation therapy (RT), and systemic therapy may be considered.
Metastatic Recurrence
Question: How should a metastatic recurrence of chordoma be treated?
Answer: Systemic therapy, surgical excision, radiation therapy (RT), and best supportive care may be considered.
Additional Information

Targeted Therapy Decision
Question: How are targeted therapy opportunities for chordoma determined?
Answer: Consideration should be given to comprehensive genomic profiling (CGP) with a validated and/or FDA-approved assay.
TMB and MMR/MSI Testing
Question: Should TMB and MMR/MSI testing be considered in the treatment of chordoma?
Answer: Testing for TMB and MMR/MSI may be considered as determined by a validated and/or FDA-approved assay.
Metastatic Disease at Presentation

Question: Is the metastatic disease resectable or unresectable?
Resectable Metastatic Disease

Action:
Refer to OSTEO-2 for management of the primary tumor.
Consider chemotherapy, metastasectomy, stereotactic radiation therapy (RT), or ablation for pulmonary, visceral, and skeletal metastases.
Surveillance:
Follow up with surveillance according to OSTEO-4.
Unresectable Metastatic Disease

Action:
Administer chemotherapy and radiation therapy.
Reassess the primary site as appropriate for local control.
Workup for Osteosarcoma

Question: What are the workup steps for Osteosarcoma?
Answers:
History and physical examination
Contrast-enhanced MRI and CT of the primary site, chest imaging including chest CT, FDG-PET/CT, and/or bone scan
Lab tests including LDH and alkaline phosphatase (ALP)
Consider fertility consultation and genetic counseling
Primary Treatment for Osteosarcoma

For low-grade osteosarcoma, assess if it is intramedullary or surface:
If intramedullary, perform wide excision.
If surface, consider chemotherapy.
For high-grade osteosarcoma, refer to OSTEO-2 for treatment guidelines.
For metastatic disease at presentation, refer to OSTEO-3 for treatment guidelines.
For extraskeletal osteosarcoma, follow the NCCN Guidelines for Soft Tissue Sarcoma.
Adjuvant Treatment for Osteosarcoma

Question: What is the adjuvant treatment for high-grade osteosarcoma?
Answers:
For high-grade osteosarcoma, chemotherapy (category 1), followed by surveillance (OSTEO-4).
For low-grade osteosarcoma, proceed to surveillance (OSTEO-4).
Neoadjuvant Treatment for High-Grade Osteosarcoma

Question: Is the high-grade osteosarcoma intramedullary or surface?
Action: Administer preoperative chemotherapy (category 1).
Restage: Assess if the tumor is resectable after restaging.
If unresectable, proceed to adjuvant treatment.
If resectable, perform wide excision.
Postoperative Pathology: Assess if surgical margins are negative or positive.
If margins are negative and the response is good, continue with chemotherapy.
If margins are negative but the response is poor, continue the preoperative regimen or consider changing chemotherapy (category 3).
If margins are positive, consider additional local therapy (surgical resection + RT).
Workup for Ewing Sarcoma

Question: What are the recommended workup procedures for Ewing Sarcoma?
Answer:
History and physical examination
Contrast-enhanced MRI of the primary site
Chest CT
FDG-PET/CT (head-to-toe) and/or bone scan
Consider bone marrow biopsy and/or screening MRI of spine and pelvis
Cytogenetics and/or molecular studies
Lactate dehydrogenase (LDH) test
Fertility consultation
Primary Treatment for Localized Ewing Sarcoma

Question: What is the primary treatment for localized Ewing Sarcoma?
Answer: Multiagent chemotherapy for at least 9 weeks prior to local therapy.
Restaging Ewing Sarcoma

Question: What are the procedures for restaging Ewing Sarcoma?
Answer:
Restage with chest CT
Contrast-enhanced MRI plus CT (without contrast) of the primary site
X-rays of the primary site
Consider FDG-PET/CT (head-to-toe) or bone scan
Repeat other abnormal studies
Response Assessment for Ewing Sarcoma

Localized Disease
Question: How is the response to treatment assessed for localized Ewing Sarcoma?
Answer: Refer to the EW-2 section for response evaluation criteria.
Metastatic Disease
Question: How is the response to treatment assessed for metastatic Ewing Sarcoma?
Answer: Refer to the EW-3 section for response evaluation criteria.
Progressive Disease
Question: What are the treatment options for progressive Ewing Sarcoma?
Answer: Refer to the EW-3 section for response evaluation criteria.
Post-Primary Treatment Surveillance for Ewing Sarcoma

Question: What are the surveillance options post-treatment?
Answers:
Regular physical exam, contrast-enhanced MRI & CT of the primary site
Chest imaging every 2-3 months, CBC, and other labs as indicated
After 24 months, increase intervals for exams and imaging indefinitely
Consider FDG-PET/CT or bone scan as clinically indicated
Treatment for Progressive Disease or Relapse in Ewing Sarcoma

Question: What is the treatment approach for progressive disease or relapse?
Answers:
Chemotherapy with or without RT and surgery
Metastatic Ewing Sarcoma Treatment

Local Control Therapy
Question: Are the surgical margins positive after tumor excision?
Answers:
If yes, continue chemotherapy followed by RT
If no, definitive RT and chemotherapy
Widely Metastatic Ewing Sarcoma
Question: What are the treatment options for widely metastatic Ewing Sarcoma?
Answers:
Continue chemotherapy with palliative surgery
Palliative RT to symptomatic areas
Other techniques for multiple metastases
Adjuvant and Additional Therapy for Ewing Sarcoma

Question: What is the follow-up treatment after local control therapy with positive margins?
Answers:
If margins are positive, continue chemotherapy followed by RT
If margins are negative, chemotherapy (category 1)
Treatment of Metastases in Ewing Sarcoma

Question: How should oligometastatic or lung-only metastatic Ewing Sarcoma be treated?
Answers:
Resection of metastases or RT for oligometastatic disease
Resection plus whole lung irradiation (WLI) for lung-only partial response
Consider whole lung irradiation for lung-only complete response
Surveillance

Description: Routine follow-up for osteosarcoma post-treatment
Actions for Surveillance

Physical Exam and Imaging

Perform physical exam and imaging of the primary site and chest.
Follow-Up Schedule

Every 3 months for year 1 and 2
Every 4 months for year 3
Every 6 months for year 4 and 5
Yearly thereafter, as clinically indicated
Laboratory Studies

Conduct complete blood count (CBC) and other laboratory studies as clinically indicated.
Advanced Imaging

Consider FDG-PET/CT (head-to-toe) and/or bone scan (category 2B).
Function Assessment

Reassess function at every visit.


Relapse

Description: Treatment options upon osteosarcoma relapse
Actions for Relapse

Chemotherapy and Resection

Administer chemotherapy and/or resection if possible.
Imaging to Assess Response

Conduct imaging to assess response, including X-rays, contrast-enhanced CT & MRI of local sites, and Chest CT.
Options for Relapse Progression

If there is relapse or progression, consider the following:
Resection, if possible
Participation in a clinical trial
Palliative radiation therapy (RT)
Best supportive care
